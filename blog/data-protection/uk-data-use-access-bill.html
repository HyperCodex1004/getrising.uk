The UK's Data (Use and Access) Bill: What It Means for Privacy, Innovation, and Compliance

The UK is entering a new era of data protection with the introduction of the Data (Use and Access) Bill. Proposed in late 2024 and gaining momentum in 2025, this legislation aims to reshape how personal data is used, accessed, and regulated in the United Kingdom. As the country moves further from the EU's GDPR framework, businesses and privacy advocates alike are paying close attention.

But what exactly does the new bill mean for your organization's compliance strategy—and for the future of privacy and innovation?

🔄 A Shift Toward "Smarter" Data Regulation

The Data (Use and Access) Bill is positioned as a modernization of the UK’s data laws. It seeks to balance two goals:

  • Promoting innovation and economic growth by reducing regulatory friction
  • Maintaining strong privacy protections for individuals

This approach reflects a desire to create a "UK-specific" data governance model—one that diverges slightly from GDPR without losing essential protections.

Key areas of reform include:

  • Streamlining Subject Access Requests (SARs)
  • Redefining "scientific research" to allow broader reuse of data
  • Adjusting consent requirements for secondary data uses
  • Enhancing the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) powers and focus

🔬 Key Changes and What They Mean

✅ 1. Easing the Burden of Subject Access Requests

Organizations will have more flexibility to refuse or charge a fee for "vexatious or excessive" SARs. This change is intended to curb abuse while still ensuring legitimate access rights.

Impact: Businesses can manage SAR workloads more effectively, but must still justify refusals carefully to avoid non-compliance.

✅ 2. Redefining "Scientific Research"

The Bill broadens the definition of scientific research, making it easier for organizations to reuse personal data for innovative projects without needing fresh consent every time.

Impact: Companies engaged in R&D, data analytics, and AI development will find it simpler to leverage existing datasets—provided appropriate safeguards are in place.

✅ 3. Consent and Data Reuse

The Bill proposes lighter consent requirements for secondary data processing if the new purpose is "compatible" with the original.

Impact: Organizations can innovate faster, but must conduct thorough compatibility assessments and maintain transparency with data subjects.

✅ 4. Empowering the ICO

The ICO will shift toward a more "business-friendly" regulator model, with greater emphasis on supporting compliant innovation and proactive advice.

Impact: Expect more guidance and collaboration opportunities—but also a higher bar for accountability when breaches or complaints arise.

🔥 Opportunities and Risks

While the Data (Use and Access) Bill promises to streamline compliance, it also introduces new grey areas that could expose organizations to risk if not navigated carefully.

Opportunities:

  • Easier cross-sector data collaboration
  • Simplified compliance processes for smaller businesses
  • Competitive advantage for early adopters

Risks:

  • Misinterpreting "compatible purpose" could trigger enforcement actions
  • International data transfers may face new scrutiny if divergence from GDPR grows
  • Public trust could erode if changes are seen as weakening privacy rights

🛡️ Preparing for the Transition

To stay ahead of the curve, organizations should:

  • Review and update privacy policies to reflect the new definitions and rights
  • Audit data processing activities for "secondary use" compatibility
  • Train staff on the nuances of SAR handling under the new rules
  • Monitor ICO guidance closely as the law is finalized and implemented

📈 Final Thought

The UK's Data (Use and Access) Bill signals a new chapter in the evolution of data protection. By embracing innovation while safeguarding individual rights, businesses can turn regulatory change into an opportunity—not just a compliance headache.

Staying informed, proactive, and privacy-conscious will be the key to success in this new landscape.
