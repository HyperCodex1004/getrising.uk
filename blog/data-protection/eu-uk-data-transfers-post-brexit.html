EU-UK Data Transfers Post-Brexit: Navigating Adequacy and Compliance

If the adequacy decision is revoked or altered, it could significantly complicate how UK organizations transfer personal data to and from Europe.

An adequacy decision means the European Commission recognizes that a country‚Äôs data protection laws provide essentially equivalent safeguards to those of the EU.

In 2021, the UK received such a decision post-Brexit‚Äîallowing personal data to continue flowing freely between the UK and EU without needing extra safeguards (like SCCs or Binding Corporate Rules).

Several factors could put the UK's adequacy at risk:

  • Divergence from GDPR: With the new Data (Use and Access) Bill, the UK is modifying its data protection framework.
  • Concerns over surveillance practices: EU courts are increasingly strict about government access to personal data.
  • Political tensions: Broader UK-EU political dynamics could spill over into data protection decisions.

If adequacy lapses, UK organizations will have to implement alternative legal mechanisms for EU data transfers‚Äîand fast.

Without adequacy, cross-border data flows will require:

  • Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs): Updated templates must be signed with each EU partner.
  • Transfer Impact Assessments (TIAs): Organizations must assess whether SCCs provide sufficient protection.
  • Possibly Data Localization: Some EU companies might insist on keeping data inside the EU entirely.

The administrative burden, legal costs, and potential delays could be significant‚Äîespecially for SMEs.
